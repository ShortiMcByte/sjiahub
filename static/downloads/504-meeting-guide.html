Getting sick can be very serious and may result in a severe flare-up including hospitalization. The school needs to understand what to watch for and how to respond.

Note: Anti-inflammatory medications (e.g. naproxen/Aleve) can mask underlying symptoms including muscle pain and fever. A reading that seems low may actually indicate a flare.

Signs to Watch For

These signs may indicate a flare-up is starting or already underway:

  • ☐Raccoon eyes, redness or discoloration on the eyelids
  • ☐Limp when walking or difficulty with gait
  • ☐Body aches, particularly complaining that legs hurt
  • ☐Low fever, if above 99.5 F while on medication, this can signal a flare
  • ☐Rash, usually under arms and upper legs, but may appear as red splotches on face or joints
  • ☐Shortness of breath
  • ☐Heart palpitations
  • ☐Fatigue or unusual tiredness

Emergency vs. Non-Emergency Response

Call 911 / Contact Parents Immediately

  • ☐Shortness of breath that does not resolve in 5-10 minutes, lips turning blue, or inability to talk
  • ☐Heart palpitations
  • ☐Severe pain, significant limp, or inability to walk
  • ☐High fever over 101.5 F
  • ☐Severe or noticeable swelling (if larger than a golf ball, call 911)

Note: Anxiety attacks can also cause shortness of breath and heart palpitations. If you know the child is anxious, try calming techniques first, but still contact parents.

Contact Parents (Non-Emergency)

  • ☐Complaining of pain or discomfort, showing a limp
  • ☐Minor swelling in any joints
  • ☐Low fever between 99.5 and 101.5 F

What We Need From the School

  • ☐Allow periodic breaks to stand, stretch, and move around (staying seated too long causes stiffness)
  • ☐Allow alternating between sitting and standing during the day
  • ☐Option to sit on the floor or carpet for work when comfortable
  • ☐Allow flexible seating positions (knees up, cross-legged with knees in shirt)
  • ☐Access to a blanket in class (sensitivity to cold)
  • ☐Permission to have a cushioned or more comfortable chair (family will provide)
  • ☐Permission to snack as needed (steroid medications increase appetite)
  • ☐Ensure adequate water intake throughout the day (helps with medication-related dizziness)
  • ☐Modified or low-impact participation in gym and physical activities during flare-ups
  • ☐Reduced walking distances when experiencing leg inflammation or gait issues
  • ☐Excused absences for flare-ups, medical appointments, and lab work
  • ☐Separation from visibly sick students or during known outbreaks (especially if on immunosuppressants)
  • ☐Teachers periodically check in on how the child is feeling (may not speak up on their own)
  • ☐Minimize drawing attention to accommodations (anxiety about being singled out)

Questions to Ask at the Meeting

  1. What is the process for my child to take a break when needed without disrupting class?
  2. Who will be trained to recognize the signs of a flare-up?
  3. How will substitute teachers be informed about these accommodations?
  4. What is the plan for making up work missed during absences or medical appointments?
  5. Can we arrange a comfortable seating option, and can we provide it ourselves?
  6. How will gym and recess be handled during a flare-up or when mobility is limited?
  7. What is the protocol if there is a contagious illness outbreak in the classroom?
  8. How can teachers discreetly check in without drawing peer attention?
  9. When and how will we review and update this 504 plan?

Important: Children with sJIA may try to mask or hide symptoms because they are tired of being sick, scared of hospitalization, or anxious about attention. Teachers play a critical role in watching for subtle signs and gently checking in.
